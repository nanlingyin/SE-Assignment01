## Part 2 - Quick Overview of the 6 Models (~4 minutes)

First, I will give a brief overview of the six models.

### 1. Waterfall Model

The Waterfall Model is a traditional and linear software development model.
It divides development into fixed stages such as requirements, design, implementation, testing, and maintenance.
Each stage must be completed before moving to the next one.

This model is best used when project requirements are very clear and unlikely to change.
A good example is government or banking systems with strict documentation and approval processes.

### 2. Agile Methodology

Agile is an iterative and flexible approach to software development.
Instead of completing the whole project at once, Agile breaks work into smaller cycles and improves the product continuously through feedback.

Agile is suitable for projects where requirements may change often.
A common real-world example is software companies developing web or mobile applications.

### 3. Scrum Framework

Scrum is a framework within Agile.
It organizes work into short time periods called sprints, usually lasting two to four weeks.
It also defines roles such as Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Development Team.

Scrum is useful for teams that need regular planning, communication, and feedback.
It is widely used by software teams in companies like Microsoft and many startup teams.

### 4. Kanban

Kanban is a visual workflow management method.
It uses a board with columns such as "To Do," "In Progress," and "Done" to help teams track tasks and control work in progress.

Kanban works well for teams that need continuous delivery and flexible task management.
It is often used in support teams, maintenance teams, and product development teams.

### 5. DevOps

DevOps is both a culture and a set of practices that connect software development and IT operations.
Its goal is to improve collaboration, automate deployment, and deliver software faster and more reliably.

DevOps is suitable for projects that require frequent releases and stable system operations.
Companies like Amazon, Netflix, and Google use DevOps practices extensively.

### 6. Spiral Model

The Spiral Model is a risk-driven development model.
It combines elements of design, prototyping, and risk analysis in repeated cycles.
Each cycle helps the team identify risks and improve the system step by step.

This model is most suitable for large, complex, and high-risk projects.
It is often associated with aerospace, defense, and research-based software systems.

## Part 3 - Agile vs Waterfall (~2 minutes)

Next, I will compare Agile and Waterfall, because they are two of the most well-known development approaches.

The main difference is how they handle planning and change.

In the Waterfall Model, the project is planned in detail from the beginning.
The team follows a fixed sequence of stages, and changes are usually difficult and expensive once development has started.
This makes Waterfall more suitable for projects with stable requirements.

In contrast, Agile accepts that change is normal.
The team develops the product in small iterations, collects feedback regularly, and adjusts the plan along the way.
This makes Agile more suitable for dynamic projects where customer needs may evolve over time.

Another difference is customer involvement.
In Waterfall, customers are usually involved at the beginning and at the end.
In Agile, customers or stakeholders are involved much more frequently throughout the process.

Documentation is also different.
Waterfall usually requires heavy documentation, while Agile focuses more on working software and team collaboration.

In my opinion, Waterfall is better when the work must follow strict procedures, while Agile is better when flexibility and quick response are important.

## Part 4 - Comparison Table Walkthrough (~2-3 minutes)

Now I will walk through my comparison table and highlight several important differences.

### First difference: Flexibility

The most flexible models are Agile, Scrum, and Kanban.
These models allow teams to adjust priorities and respond to changes quickly.
Waterfall is the least flexible because it depends on completing one phase before the next.
The Spiral Model is also flexible, but mainly in terms of managing risks and refining the system in cycles.

### Second difference: Customer involvement

Agile and Scrum usually have high customer involvement, because feedback is collected frequently.
Kanban can also support regular customer feedback, depending on how the team works.
Waterfall generally has lower customer involvement after the requirements phase.
DevOps focuses more on collaboration between development and operations, but it also helps improve user experience through faster releases.

### Third difference: Documentation level

Waterfall usually has the highest level of documentation.
This is useful for formal projects with strict control.
Agile, Scrum, and Kanban usually have lighter documentation, because they focus more on communication and working results.
DevOps documentation often focuses on automation, pipelines, deployment, and monitoring.
The Spiral Model may also require strong documentation because of its focus on analysis and risk control.

### Fourth difference: Best suited project type

Waterfall is best for stable and predictable projects.
Agile and Scrum are ideal for fast-changing software projects.
Kanban is good for continuous workflow and maintenance tasks.
DevOps is best for products that need frequent deployment and reliable operations.
The Spiral Model is best for large and risky projects.

## Part 5 - Which Model I Would Choose (~1-2 minutes)

If I had to choose one model for a student team project, I would choose **Scrum**.

The first reason is that Scrum gives a clear structure.
Student teams often need deadlines, short-term goals, and regular meetings to stay organized.
Sprints make the work easier to divide into manageable parts.

The second reason is communication.
Scrum encourages regular discussion, which helps team members solve problems early and keep everyone aligned.

The third reason is flexibility.
In student projects, ideas and requirements often change during development.
Scrum allows the team to improve the product step by step without losing control.

Overall, I think Scrum is practical, easy to apply, and very suitable for small collaborative teams.

## Part 6 - Conclusion (~1 minute)

In conclusion, each software development model has its own strengths and weaknesses.

Waterfall is structured and documentation-focused.
Agile is flexible and iterative.
Scrum provides a clear framework for Agile teamwork.
Kanban improves workflow visibility.
DevOps connects development and operations for faster delivery.
The Spiral Model is useful for complex and high-risk systems.

There is no single best model for every project.
The best choice depends on project size, risk level, customer involvement, and how often requirements change.
